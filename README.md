Raising the antenna increases the horizon distance and provides more vertical space to clear obstacles, reducing the risk of signal diffraction.2️⃣ Increase Antenna Gain to 5 dBiHow does the power curve change? The entire curve shifts upward. By increasing both Gtx and Grx to 5 dBi, we add a total of 6 dB to the link budget.Additional range? The range at which the signal stays above sensitivity significantly increases (often doubling the coverage area in some environments).Why? Higher gain antennas focus the radio energy into a narrower beam, increasing the power density in the desired direction without increasing the transmitter's battery consumption.3️⃣ Change Environment Exponent ($n$)How does increasing $n$ affect coverage? Increasing $n$ from 2.0 (open space) to 4.0 (dense urban) causes the received power to drop off much more rapidly.Strongest impact? Environment change ($n$) has a much stronger impact than antenna gain.Physical explanation: Gain adds a constant value (addition), but the exponent $n$ represents the rate of decay over distance (multiplication/power). In a dense city ($n=4$), even the best antennas cannot overcome the massive path loss.4️⃣ Move Gateway Location (Fresnel Study)Largest Fresnel radius? The radius is largest at the midpoint ($0.5D$).Why is midpoint critical? The Fresnel zone is ellipsoid; it is widest in the center. An obstacle at the midpoint blocks the largest portion of the wavefront.Violation effects? If 60% clearance is violated, the signal suffers from diffraction loss, meaning the received power will be much lower than predicted by simple path loss models, potentially killing the link.Final ConclusionIn practical wireless deployments, the Environment Exponent ($n$) has the strongest impact on coverage. While an engineer can control hardware (gain and height), the physical environment (buildings, trees, terrain) dictates how fast signal energy dissipates.
